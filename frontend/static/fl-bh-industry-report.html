Section 1

Data Source & Caveats

What is the DOGE Database?

This report is built from the CMS DOGE database, which publishes aggregated Medicaid/Medicare payment data at the billing NPI × HCPCS code × month grain. We isolate Florida by matching billing NPIs to NPPES provider addresses in FL. We scope to 22 outpatient behavioral health HCPCS codes.

Why the gap? DOGE captures ~40–50% of national OP BH Medicaid spend because it covers only fee-for-service equivalent claims visible at the HCPCS level. 1c. ABA — Excluded ($234M FL Market)

Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) is NOT in scope. ABA services (CPT 97151–97158, 0362T–0374T) represent $234M in FL Medicaid revenue in DOGE — a rapidly growing segment driven by autism spectrum disorder mandates. Many "BH_SPECIALTY" new entrants in Section 6 are primarily ABA providers who also bill some of our 22 BH codes. Their BH revenue in this report significantly understates their true scale. ABA is excluded because it has fundamentally different service delivery models, payer dynamics, and competitive structure from traditional BH.

1d. What is NOT in DOGE

Any of these gaps will make organizations appear smaller than actual:
  • Solo practitioner therapy — The largest gap. Individual therapists bill under Type 1 NPIs, not captured in our org-level analysis.
  • Residential/inpatient per-diems — Facility revenue codes (0100-0169) not in DOGE.
  • Managed care capitation — FL SMMC capitated payments invisible.
  • SUD medications (MAT) — Buprenorphine, methadone under different codes.
  • Beneficiary double-counting — One patient using 3 codes = counted 3 times. Cross-NPI attribution not possible.
  • Commercial/private insurance — Only Medicaid/Medicare visible.

1e. 2024 Data Completeness

National 2024 data is complete through ~May, then progressively understated (Dec at 22% of expected). This report uses 2023 as the latest complete year.

1f. Billing Unit Caveat

Organizations bill the same HCPCS code at different granularities (per 15-minute unit vs per encounter vs per diem). This means rate-per-claim is not comparable across orgs. This report uses revenue per beneficiary (RPB) as the primary value metric.

Section 2

Metric Definitions

All metrics are computed from the 22 BH HCPCS codes unless otherwise noted. "Org" refers to a consolidated billing entity identified by one or more billing NPIs registered to the same legal name in NPPES.

MetricDefinitionUnit
RevenueSum of total_paid across all BH codes for the entity$ (annual)
BeneficiariesSum of beneficiary_count per code per month. Represents service contacts, not unique patients (one patient using 3 codes = counted 3 times)Count
ClaimsSum of claim_count. Unit varies by org billing practice (15-min vs encounter vs day)Count
Revenue per Beneficiary (RPB)Total paid / beneficiary count for a given code. Primary value metric — normalizes for billing granularity$/bene/year
Claims per Beneficiary (CPB)Total claims / beneficiary count. Reflects service intensity/frequencyClaims/bene/year
Service MixClaim share by HCPCS code (org claims for code X / org total BH claims). Shows which services dominate%
Market ShareOrg BH claims in catchment ZIPs / all BH claims in those ZIPs. Catchment = ZIPs where org has billing NPIs%
5-Factor DecompositionRevenue change decomposed into: Beneficiaries × Utilization × Rate × Mix × Interaction. Each as an index relative to base year (2020 = 1.000)Index

Decomposition Method

We use a Paasche-weighted rate/mix decomposition. Rate index uses current-year service mix weights, so new codes (e.g., H0040 appearing after 2020) are correctly attributed. Mix is the residual: mix = rpc_ratio / rate. Interaction captures only the bene×rate cross-term and is typically 1.000.

Why Paasche, not Laspeyres? A standard Laspeyres index uses base-year weights for rate computation. When new service codes are introduced (weight=0 in base year), all their revenue impact falls into the interaction term, making it uninterpretable. Paasche uses current-year weights, properly attributing new-code effects and keeping interaction negligible.

Section 8

Narrative & Interpretation

Executive Summary

Florida’s organizational BH Medicaid market (DOGE-visible, 22 codes) was $366M in 2023 — a partial window into an estimated $1.5–2B true market. Within this window, 1,203 classified organizations compete across 7 archetypes. One structural story dominates: CMHCs are consolidating share at the expense of value.

Validated Thesis: The CMHC Structural Trap

We tested four hypotheses against the DOGE data. All four are confirmed by the evidence:

Thesis 1: CMHCs are underpaid — and the gap is worsening

We separate rate analysis into two metrics to avoid billing-unit distortion:

  • Rate per Claim (RPC) — for per-encounter codes (90837, 90834, 90832, H0031, H0032, H0001) where 1 claim = 1 session
  • Revenue per Beneficiary (RPB) — for time/unit/diem codes (H2019, T1017, H0040) where claim counts depend on billing granularity

The Structural Trap

Combining all four theses: CMHCs are in a structural trap. They are gaining market share (17pp since 2019) by absorbing volume from COMMUNITY_BH, but at a worsening RPB discount (−25%). Their service mix is shifting toward intake+therapy and away from community services, which means they are becoming more like expensive assessment centers and less like community-based safety nets. The RPB gap has widened post-COVID, not narrowed.

This is the opposite of a virtuous cycle: more volume → stretched capacity → lower per-bene extraction → need more volume to maintain revenue → even more stretched capacity.

Section 9

Strategic Imperatives

The data validates four structural challenges for CMHCs. Each requires a distinct intervention:

1. Fix the Rate (RPB Gap: −25%)

Problem: CMHC RPB is $83 vs $110 market. The gap worsened from −22% (2020) to −25% (2023).
Root causes to investigate: (a) Billing unit mismatch — are CMHCs billing per 15-min unit while peers bill per encounter? (b) MCO rate negotiation — are CMHCs accepting lower contracted rates in exchange for volume guarantees? (c) Case mix — are CMHCs serving less intensive cases on codes like H2019?
Action: Code-level RPB benchmarking against archetype-matched peers. Identify which codes drive the gap and whether it is a billing practice, contracting, or clinical model issue. The H2019 −60% RPB gap alone represents a $47M+ opportunity.

2. Fix the Leakage (Intake Capture Without Downstream Retention)

Problem: CMHC intake share grew to 21.8% of revenue while community services dropped to 45.9%. CMHCs are assessing patients but not retaining them for ongoing treatment.
Evidence: CMHC intake RPB is +8% above market (they win the front door), but overall RPB is −25% (they lose on ongoing care). COMMUNITY_BH providers maintain 87% community service mix — they are capturing the downstream.
Action: Track conversion ratios (intake benes → ongoing treatment benes) by code and compare to COMMUNITY_BH benchmarks. If patients are leaving after assessment, the question is whether it is capacity constraints, wait times, or MCO network routing.

3. Fix the Productivity (Volume Growth Without Margin)

Problem: CMHC revenue share doubled (16.6% → 33.6%) but RPB dropped 19% ($103 → $83). Volume growth is masking deteriorating unit economics.
Evidence: COMMUNITY_BH lost 18.5pp of share and shrank −17% in absolute revenue. CMHCs absorbed this volume but at lower per-bene economics. This is the structural trap: more patients → lower intensity per patient → lower RPB → need more patients.
Action: Separate organic growth (same patients, more services) from absorptive growth (new patients, same or lower services). If absorptive, CMHCs need to ensure new patients receive the same service intensity as existing ones, or explicitly price the lower-intensity model with MCOs.

4. Think Network, Not Isolation

Problem: CMHCs are trying to be everything — intake, therapy, community services, case management — and losing margin on all of it. Meanwhile, COMMUNITY_BH providers focus narrowly on community services at 87%+ mix and maintain higher RPB.
Opportunity: CMHCs are natural network hubs. Their intake premium (+8%) and breadth of services positions them as care coordinators, not just providers. The value proposition to MCOs should be: CMHCs as the BH medical home that routes patients to the right level of care (including COMMUNITY_BH partners) while retaining the high-acuity cases where they outperform (ACT/FACT +12%, case management +10%).
Action: Build formal referral networks with COMMUNITY_BH providers for ongoing service delivery. Use data to demonstrate care coordination value rather than competing on unit cost.
Bottom line: The data shows CMHCs winning share in a market that is compressing their rates. The gap worsened post-COVID vs the 2020 baseline. The four interventions — fix rates, fix leakage, fix productivity, think network — are not independent. A CMHC that fixes its H2019 RPB gap (+$47M potential), retains post-intake patients, and positions itself as a BH network hub can break the structural trap. Without intervention, the current trajectory leads to higher volume at lower margins indefinitely.
